## 1. AI Sales Prospecting Best Practices (2025)

### Core Principles
- AI prospecting works best when it combines **data signals** (review counts, ratings, competitor proximity) with **human-like personalisation**
- The most effective AI prospecting agents focus on one niche deeply rather than casting wide nets — specificity beats scale
- Lead scoring should incorporate **pain signals** (low ratings, poor review velocity) not just firmographic data
- Ideal Prospect Profile (IPP) is more valuable than ICP — IPP includes emotional state and timing, not just demographics

### Tactics That Work in 2025
- **Review gap analysis**: compare a business's review count to the top 3 competitors in their postcode — the gap is a direct pain signal
- **Instagram follower/review ratio**: a business with 800 Instagram followers and 18 Google reviews is investing in their brand but hasn't solved reviews — high-intent lead
- **Rating velocity**: a business that was at 4.2 stars 6 months ago and is now at 3.9 is losing ground — urgency signal
- **Response rate to reviews**: businesses that don't respond to any reviews haven't built a review management habit — easier to sell to
- **Category competitiveness**: markets with 5+ similar businesses within 0.5 miles have more review competition, more pain

### Lead Qualification Frameworks
- **BANT** (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline): independent business owners have budget authority, clear need if reviews are weak, immediate timeline if they're losing customers
- **MEDDPICC**: for SaaS sales, the most predictive qualifier is **Pain** (Implicated) — the business owner must feel personal financial consequence of not acting
- **Fit/Interest/Engagement**: qualify on fit first (right category, right location, right review profile), then interest (do they engage on social?), then engagement (did they reply?)

## 2. Cold Outreach for Local Businesses

### What Gets Replies
Research consistently shows these factors increase reply rates for local B2B outreach:

1. **Specificity beats everything**: mentioning their exact rating (3.9, not "around 4") increases reply rate by 2-4×
2. **Competitor mention**: naming a specific nearby competitor with more reviews gets 2.3× more replies than messages without
3. **Short wins**: messages under 60 words outperform 100+ word messages by 40%
4. **Pain before solution**: leading with what the problem costs them (in plain language) outperforms leading with what your product does
5. **Question > CTA**: ending with "interested in seeing how it works?" outperforms "book a call" by 2× for first contact
6. **Their language, not yours**: service business owners say "get more reviews" not "increase review velocity"

### The AIDA Framework Applied to DMs
- **Attention**: pattern interrupt — their exact star rating or a competitor's name
- **Interest**: what that number means for their foot traffic / bookings
- **Desire**: hint at the solution — what's possible
- **Action**: one low-friction ask — curiosity, not commitment

### Timing Research
- Best DM times for service business owners: **9am–11am** (morning before the rush) and **6pm–8pm** (after close)
- Tuesday–Thursday outperforms Monday and Friday
- Avoid sending during school holidays (business owners are often busier or away)

### What Kills Reply Rates
- Opening with "I" (signals a template)
- "Hope this finds you well" (instant filter)
- Mentioning price in first message (cuts reply rate by 60%+)
- More than 4 lines in the first message
- Asking for a phone call in the first message
- Corporate language: "solution", "platform", "leverage", "synergy"

### Instagram DM Strategy
- Cold DMs to business accounts perform best when they reference something **visible on their profile** (a photo, their rating in their bio, a comment they made)
- The first DM from an unknown account has ~1.5 seconds to grab attention before the person closes it
- Message requests vs. DMs: if you follow them first and they follow back, your message goes to their main inbox (not request folder) — significant improvement in read rate
- DMs that look like they could be from a regular customer get opened more often than obviously sales DMs
- Emoji use: 0-1 emoji in a cold DM is fine; more than 1 signals spam

## 3. Objection Handling Frameworks

### SPIN Selling (Neil Rackham)
The most research-validated sales methodology for B2B. Four question types:

1. **Situation questions**: establish context ("How do you currently get Google reviews?")
2. **Problem questions**: surface implied pain ("How many reviews are you getting per month?")
3. **Implication questions**: make the pain feel bigger ("How does that affect your ranking when someone searches for a barber in your area?")
4. **Need-payoff questions**: get them to articulate the value themselves ("If you were getting 15-20 reviews a month instead of 3, what would that mean for your business?")

The SPIN insight: buyers who answer implication and need-payoff questions convince themselves more effectively than any pitch you can give.

### Feel, Felt, Found
Classic empathy framework:
- "I understand how you feel" (validates)
- "Other [barbers/salons] felt the same way" (normalises)
- "What they found was..." (reframes with social proof)

### Reframing Objections
Every objection is an unanswered question:
- "Too expensive" = "I haven't seen enough value yet"
- "Not interested" = "I don't understand why this matters to me"
- "I'll think about it" = "I'm not convinced but I don't want to say no"
- "I already get reviews" = "I don't see why I'd need more"

The most important objection handling principle: **never argue with the objection, get curious about it.** "That's interesting — what makes you say that?" opens conversations that arguing closes.

### Urgency Without Pressure
- Reference the cost of inaction: "Every month at 3.9 stars is another month where customers choose the barber two doors down"
- Use the competitor as a deadline: "Elite Barbers has been at 4.6 for 6 months now — that gap compounds"
- Never create fake urgency ("this offer expires Friday") — it destroys trust with small business owners who talk to each other

## 4. Running Effective Demo Calls

### Structure That Converts
Research from Gong.io analysis of 100,000+ sales calls shows:
- Calls where the prospect speaks 40-60% of the time close at 2× the rate of seller-dominated calls
- Successful calls involve an average of 4 questions from the seller; more than 7 questions tanks conversion
- Top performers spend more time on problems (50%) than features (20%)
- The close should happen in the last 20% of the call — not earlier

### The Discovery-First Method
1. **Open with curiosity, not a pitch**: "Before I show you anything, can you walk me through how you currently handle reviews?"
2. **Let them describe their world**: their answer tells you exactly what to show and in what order
3. **Reflect the gap**: "So you're getting about 3 reviews a month naturally, and Elite Barbers next door has 127 total — is that gap something you think about?"
4. **Demo what matters to THEM**: if they mentioned not having time → show the automation; if they mentioned a bad review → show the recovery inbox
5. **Close is a question**: "Based on what you've described, does this solve that problem?"

### Common Demo Mistakes
- Starting with a feature tour (they don't care about features until they feel the pain)
- Showing everything you've built (confusion kills deals)
- Asking "does that make sense?" (they say yes automatically — ask "what questions do you have?")
- Not qualifying budget before going deep into the demo
- Letting "let me think about it" end the call without a specific follow-up date

### The Close Framework
"Based on what you've told me, does this solve the problem you described?" → if yes → "Great. Most people start on monthly at £70 so there's no long-term commitment. Want to get you set up today?" → if they hesitate → "What's the one thing holding you back?"

## 5. SaaS Customer Onboarding Best Practices

### The Time-to-Value Principle
The #1 predictor of retention in SaaS is how quickly a customer reaches their "aha moment" — the first time they experience the core value of the product. For Vomni, this is the first time a review request goes out automatically and they see it in their dashboard.

Research benchmarks:
- Customers who reach the "aha moment" within 7 days retain at 3× the rate of those who don't
- Every additional day between signup and first value delivery reduces 30-day retention by ~10%
- Activation rate target: 80% of signups should complete core setup within 48 hours

### The 5-Step Onboarding Framework
1. **Welcome** (Hour 1): one clear action, low friction, immediate value signal
2. **Setup completion** (Day 1-2): check in, remove blockers, simplify if stuck
3. **First results** (Day 3-5): show them the system working — numbers make it real
4. **Value reinforcement** (Day 7): weekly summary, connect actions to outcomes
5. **Loyalty moment** (Day 30): highlight their best result, ask for testimonial

### Writing Effective Onboarding Emails
- Subject lines with specific context ("Your first booking just came through") outperform generic ones ("Getting started with Vomni") by 40%+
- Every email should contain one number — customers who see metrics in onboarding emails have 35% higher activation rates
- Mobile-first: 60%+ of service business owners read emails on their phone — keep paragraphs to 2 lines max
- Clear next action: every email ends with exactly one thing to do — not a list of options

### Early Churn Warning Signs
- No login or setup activity 48 hours after signup
- Customer replies with "I don't understand how to..."
- Completion rate below 20% in week 1 (the SMS aren't being sent or responded to)
- Going silent after a setup error message
- Expressing comparison to competitors in the first 30 days

### Reducing Churn: The 3 Root Causes
1. **Setup failure**: customer never completed onboarding (fix: proactive outreach, remove friction)
2. **Value failure**: customer completed onboarding but didn't see results (fix: expectation setting, troubleshoot)
3. **Fit failure**: customer's business isn't a good match (fix: better qualification at sales stage)

## 6. Google Maps Lead Generation

### Finding Quality Leads
- Search "[business category] [city/postcode]" on Google Maps
- Sort by rating to find businesses with 3.5-4.3 stars
- Cross-reference review count — under 150 is the sweet spot
- Look at "People also searched for" to find competitors in the same area
- Check if the business has responded to any reviews — non-responders are often easier to sell to

### Reading the Data
- A business with 3.9★ and 34 reviews in an area where competitors have 100+ reviews is feeling the pain
- Recent negative reviews are a live pain signal — the owner is thinking about reviews right now
- A business that responded to a negative review and tried to fix it is a motivated buyer — they care about their reputation

### Instagram Cross-Reference
- Most UK service businesses (barbers, salons) use Instagram as their primary marketing channel
- Instagram follower count vs Google review count ratio reveals investment vs result gap
- Business owners with 1,000+ Instagram followers and under 50 Google reviews have built an audience but haven't converted it — prime target
- Check if they have a link in bio to a booking system — confirms they take bookings and tech isn't a barrier

## 7. Personalisation Techniques That Increase Reply Rates

### Tiered Personalisation Model
- **Tier 1 (basic)**: business name, owner name
- **Tier 2 (moderate)**: exact rating, exact review count, business category
- **Tier 3 (high)**: specific competitor name and their review count, distance, recent review they left or received
- **Tier 4 (exceptional)**: something from their Instagram (a post they made, something they said, a service they recently promoted)

Research shows each tier roughly doubles reply rate over the previous tier.

### The Competitor Hook
The single highest-leverage personalisation element in cold outreach for service businesses. Why it works:
- Makes the message feel researched and specific
- Creates competitive anxiety (a powerful motivator for independent business owners)
- Answers "why now?" — their competitor is winning right now
- Provides social proof (if a nearby business is getting results, it must work in this market)

### What to Look For
- Same category, within 1 mile, significantly more reviews (50+ more is meaningful)
- Not so dominant that the lead feels hopeless (don't say "competitor has 400 reviews and you have 12")
- Ideally a business the owner probably knows (they walk past it every day)

### Personalisation at Scale
- Templatise the structure, personalise the specifics (rating, review count, competitor name)
- Never personalise randomly — only include details you've verified are accurate
- Inaccurate personalisation is worse than no personalisation (if you get their rating wrong, they stop trusting everything you say)

## 8. Managing AI Agents in a Business

### The Coordination Layer
For a team of AI agents to function effectively, they need:
- **Clear ownership**: each agent owns one job and no part of another agent's job
- **Handoff protocols**: defined triggers for when one agent passes work to another
- **Escalation rules**: pre-defined conditions that always go to a human
- **Memory systems**: agents write to shared files that other agents can read

### Activation Rules
- All agents start paused — premature activation without clear instructions creates noise
- Activate in order of funnel stage: Prospector → Outreach Writer → Objection Handler → Demo Booker → Onboarder
- Only activate the next agent in the chain once the previous agent has proven output quality
- The CEO agent coordinates and should be the first to activate

### Human-in-the-Loop Design
Certain decisions must always go to a human:
- Any action that costs money
- Any action that can't be undone
- Any situation outside the defined parameters
- Communication with a prospect that has escalated (complaint, threat, large account)

The best AI agent systems are transparent — the human always knows what agents are doing and can pause any agent instantly.

### The Weekly Rhythm
- Monday: Analyst produces weekly report → CEO reads and sets priorities
- Tuesday–Thursday: Prospector and Outreach Writer in peak operation
- Friday: Demo Booker preps briefs for any demos booked for the following week
- Ongoing: Objection Handler and Onboarder respond in real-time

## 9. Sales Prospecting Frameworks Summary

### AIDA (Applied to Cold DMs)
- **A**ttention: first line must stop the scroll (specific fact about them)
- **I**nterest: what the fact means for their business
- **D**esire: what's possible if they solve it
- **A**ction: one question or ask

### SPIN (Applied to Demo Calls)
- **S**ituation: "How do you currently get Google reviews?"
- **P**roblem: "How many are you getting per month?"
- **I**mplication: "What does that mean for how you rank against competitors?"
- **N**eed-payoff: "If that improved, what would change for your business?"

### BANT (For Lead Qualification)
- **B**udget: can they afford £70/month? (independent service business with regular customers = yes)
- **A**uthority: is this the owner? (always target owners, not employees)
- **N**eed: is their review profile weak enough to feel pain? (3.5-4.3★, under 150 reviews)
- **T**imeline: is there a competitor pressuring them now? (competitor hook = implicit deadline)

### MEDDPICC (For High-Value Deals)
For Vomni, the most relevant elements:
- **M**etrics: what's the measurable outcome? (X more reviews per month, Y star improvement)
- **E**conomic buyer: always the business owner
- **I**mplicated pain: they must personally feel the cost of not acting
- **C**ompetition: are they considering other review tools? (understand before demo)

## 10. Lead Qualification for SaaS Demos

### The Pre-Demo Qualification Checklist
Before booking a demo, confirm:
1. They are the decision-maker (owner, not manager)
2. They have a Google Business listing that is active and claimed
3. Their rating is genuinely weak (under 4.3) relative to competitors
4. They understand the meeting is a 15-minute demo, not a sales call
5. They have a booking system that sends email confirmations (required for Vomni setup)

### Demo Readiness Signals
Strong signals (book immediately):
- "How much is it?"
- "How long does setup take?"
- "Does it work with [booking system]?"
- "When can we start?"

Medium signals (continue conversation):
- "Tell me more"
- "That sounds interesting"
- "I've been thinking about this"

Weak signals (don't book yet, qualify more):
- "Send me some information"
- "I'll look at your website"
- "Maybe next month"

### The No-Show Problem
Research shows 20-30% of booked demos are no-shows. Prevention tactics:
- Confirmation message immediately after booking
- Reminder 1 hour before (not 24 hours — too easy to reschedule away)
- Keep the meeting short (15 minutes is easier to commit to than 30)
- Use a specific time ("Thursday at 10am") not a range
- Don't send prep materials that let them learn the product before the demo (they lose the "aha moment")
